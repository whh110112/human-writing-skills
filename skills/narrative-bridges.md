# Narrative Bridges Skill

## Aim

Make long-form prose move by cause, perception, and changed pressure instead of by
generic transitions. Each paragraph or scene should inherit something from the
previous beat, transform it, and leave a clear reason for the next beat to exist.

## Use When

- fiction or serialized writing feels like separate generated blocks
- scene changes happen by summary instead of felt movement
- paragraphs are polished but do not answer "why now?"
- characters react in broad emotion labels instead of changing behavior, knowledge,
  leverage, distance, or risk

## Beat Chain

Before drafting a passage, define the local chain:

1. Previous residue: the image, question, injury, promise, object, line of dialogue,
   or emotional imbalance left by the prior beat.
2. Entry pressure: what forces the new beat to begin now.
3. Micro-turn: what changes inside the beat: knowledge, status, intimacy, danger,
   leverage, appetite, doubt, or self-control.
4. Exit hook: what remains unsettled, newly risky, newly desired, or newly visible.

Do not write a transition until the chain is clear.

## Techniques

- Let a concrete object or body action carry the transition before explaining it.
- Move from setting to character through attention: what the character notices first
  should reveal what they fear, want, hide, or misunderstand.
- Use time jumps only after naming the residue that survives the jump.
- When changing scenes, preserve one bridge element: a sound, physical sensation,
  unfinished question, object, threat, promise, or altered relationship.
- Prefer "because this changed" over "then this happened." A beat should alter the
  next beat's options.
- Let dialogue create a turn: a line should shift permission, distance, knowledge,
  threat, or obligation, not merely restate mood.
- Vary block size. Use short beats for shock, decision, or embarrassment; use longer
  beats when attention is narrowing, bargaining, remembering, or resisting.
- Keep one productive gap. The reader should feel a pressure to continue without
  receiving a tidy explanation after every moment.

## Avoid

- canned transitions such as "with that said," "at that moment," or "little did they know"
  when no concrete bridge exists
- opening every new paragraph with abstract summary or emotional labels
- cutting to a new scene that forgets the previous scene's cost, posture, or object
- resolving the scene's emotional meaning immediately after showing it
- adding random sensory detail that does not change attention, pressure, or choice

## Revision Check

For each scene or major paragraph, answer:

- What did the previous beat leave behind?
- What changed in this beat?
- What exact detail proves the change?
- Why must the next beat follow from this one?

If a beat can be removed without damaging cause, tension, or character state, merge it,
rewrite it, or cut it.
